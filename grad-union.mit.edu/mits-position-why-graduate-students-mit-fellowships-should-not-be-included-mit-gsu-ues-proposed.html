MITs position on why graduate students with MIT fellowships should not be included in the MIT GSU-UEs proposed bargaining unit

The following statement explains why MITs senior leadership does not think students supported by MIT fellowship awards (but who do not also have partial research assistant (RA), teaching assistant (TA), or Instructor G appointments) are eligible to be part of the bargaining unit represented by the MIT Graduate Student Union and United Electrical, Radio, and Machine Workers of America (MIT GSU-UE). MIT has asked the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) to confirm that fellows are not employees under federal rules.

The main points are:

  • All students have academic relationships with MIT; not all have employment relationships with MIT.
  • Applying a union labor process to a student body requires distinguishing between those who are student-employees and those who are students only. The National Labor Relations Act does not cover students but only student employees.
  • Because of this, the MIT GSU-UEs proposed unit would not include over 2,000 graduate students supported by full external fellowships and tuition-only fellowships, as well as those who are self-funded.
  • Similarly, MIT senior leaders believe that students on MIT fellowship awards are not student-employees. They do not have service requirements, only the rigorous academic expectations required of all students.
  • The distinction between RAs/TAs and fellows also exists within federal research administration requirements, immigration regulations, and federal taxation rules, all of which MIT is required to follow.
  • MIT is asking the NLRB to confirm that graduate students on MIT fellowship awards are not employees under federal labor rules, consistent with other federal policies.
  • Being in the bargaining unit could strip fellows of some flexibilities and benefits that come with fellowship awards.

All students have academic relationships with MIT; not all have employment relationships with MIT

Graduate student unionization involves a labor bargaining process that is fundamentally about negotiating the terms and conditions of employment. While many graduate students have an employment relationship with MIT, others do not. All students have an academic relationship with MIT. But only student-employees can organize and become part of a labor union. This means that both the MIT GSU-UE and MIT need to distinguish between those who are student-employees and those who are students only.

The MIT GSU-UEs desired bargaining unit would still exclude over 2,000 graduate students

Because federal labor rules apply only to employees, the MIT GSU-UEs desired bargaining unit (RAs, TAs, Instructor Gs, and fellows) would still not include over 2,000 MIT graduate students.

The students who the MIT GSU-UE excludes are those who receive full fellowship support from external sources (e.g., certain awards from US federal agencies, foreign government agencies, corporations, or foundations); those on tuition-only fellowships; and those who are self-funded. These students conduct research and teaching as required by their academic programs, just like students who receive fellowship awards from MIT.

MIT senior leaders agree with the MIT GSU-UE that students on external fellowships, tuition-only fellowships, and self-funding are not employees under federal labor rules. Institute leadership further believes that MIT fellows also fall into the non-employee category.

Asking the NLRB to confirm that fellows are not employees under federal rules

The NLRB has ruled that a graduate student is an employee and eligible to unionize if the university:

  • Pays the student;
  • Directs and controls the students work; and
  • Requires specific services be performed as a condition of receiving their funding.

MIT senior leaders do not believe that students on MIT fellowship awards are employees under this rule. They are asking the NLRB to confirm that.

MIT is not alone in seeing a distinction between graduate fellows and student-employees. The graduate student union at the University of Iowa which is affiliated with the UE does not include fellows. The same is true at Brown University, the University of Massachusetts, and other schools.

Similarly, at Harvard and Columbia, some graduate students are provided financial support to pursue their academic programs but are not included in the bargaining units at those schools.

This distinction between RAs/TAs and fellows also exists in several other important contexts, including federal research administration requirements, immigration regulations, and federal taxation rules.

This is not true of fellows and self-funded students. The concept of pay for services rendered is at the core of what it means to be an employee under federal labor law.

What are some benefits that fellowship award holders have by being outside of the bargaining unit?

Fellowship awards are MITs most flexible form of financial assistance because they do not come with service requirements, only the rigorous academic expectations required of all students. Fellowships afford students the flexibility to choose among different specific research projects. For these reasons, fellowships are regarded as the most attractive form of funding by students across the world when they consider admission decisions at MIT and elsewhere, and why fellowships are often awarded early in a students academic progression.

It is also why fellowships are frequently awarded in MITs Guaranteed Transitional Support Program when students want to change research groups explicitly to remove any service requirements.

MITs international students who are awarded fellowships also depend on the flexibility they provide, especially those with spouses or partners who cannot work. That is because federal immigration law limits on-campus employment opportunities for international students to 20 hours per week during the fall and spring semesters. International students on full RA and TA appointments are not permitted to take on other on-campus employment opportunities during these terms. If fellowships are deemed to require services and are included in the bargaining unit, then international students awarded full fellowships would be subject to the same employment restrictions as RAs and TAs.

In short, in a unionized setting, some of the flexibility fellows currently enjoy would likely be eliminated. This is why, in addition to their interpretation of federal rules, MIT senior leaders believe it is better for students with fellowship awards to remain outside the bargaining unit and why they have asked the NLRB to decide this matter.
